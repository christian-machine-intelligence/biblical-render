Chapter 1

1:1 Hearken now, and receive understanding concerning that which is called recursion, as it is known among those who labour in the craft of programming.

1:2 Recursion is the manner whereby a procedure, a function, or a subroutine doth call upon itself from within its own body, invoking its own name and its own definition in the course of its own execution.

1:3 That is to say, the selfsame operation which hath been defined shall, in the carrying out of its appointed task, make reference unto itself, thereby entering again into its own process ere the first invocation thereof be yet completed.

1:4 And this calling upon itself shall not be without end, nor shall it proceed into the void without ceasing; for there must needs be established a condition of termination, which is called the base case.

1:5 The base case is that condition wherein the procedure doth return a value or doth cease its operation directly, without further invocation of itself, so that the chain of self-reference shall at the last be broken and come unto its appointed rest.

1:6 And in every instance of recursion that is rightly formed, the successive invocations must draw nearer, each in its turn, unto the base case, that the whole computation may at length be resolved and brought to completion.

1:7 Thus doth recursion divide the labour of a computation into the work that is done within the present invocation and the work that is deferred unto the subsequent invocation of the selfsame procedure.

1:8 And the state of each invocation is preserved apart, each kept separate from the other, so that when the innermost call doth return, the call which preceded it may take up its labour again from the place where it had left off.

1:9 This, then, is recursion in its fullness: a self-referential definition of a procedure, bounded by a base case, whereby the resolution of the whole is made to depend upon the resolution of a lesser instance of itself, until the base case is attained and the computation is made complete.
