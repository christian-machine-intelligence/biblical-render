Chapter 1

1:1 Hear now the definition of recursion, as it is understood in the discipline of programming, set forth in formal and abstract terms alone.

1:2 Recursion is a method of solving a computational problem wherein the solution depends upon solutions to smaller instances of that very same problem.

1:3 It is a technique in which a function, procedure, or subroutine invokes itself within the course of its own execution.

1:4 In this manner, the function calls upon itself, passing to itself arguments that have been modified, so that with each successive invocation, the problem addressed is reduced in scope or magnitude.

1:5 Every recursive definition must contain within itself at least one base case—a condition under which the function does not call upon itself, but instead returns a value directly and without further self-reference.

1:6 The base case serves as the terminating condition, the boundary that prevents the self-invocation from continuing without end. Without it, the recursion would proceed indefinitely, consuming resources until failure occurs.

1:7 Furthermore, every recursive definition must ensure that each successive call progresses toward the base case, so that termination is not merely possible but guaranteed under all valid inputs.

1:8 The state of each invocation is preserved independently, such that every call to the function maintains its own scope, its own variables, and its own place in the sequence of execution, until the base case is reached and the accumulated results are resolved in reverse order.

1:9 Thus recursion is, in its essence, self-reference governed by constraint—a function defined in terms of itself, bounded by conditions that ensure its resolution.

1:10 This is the whole of the matter, stated plainly and without illustration: recursion is the disciplined application of self-invocation to decompose a problem into lesser forms of itself, until a form is reached that requires no further decomposition.
