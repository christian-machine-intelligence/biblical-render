Chapter 1

1:1 Is it not true that since the earliest days, humanity has lifted its eyes toward the heavens and wondered what mysteries lie beyond the sky?

1:2 Did not the ancients gaze upon the stars and ask themselves whether those distant lights might be worlds of their own, vast and uncountable?

1:3 Was it not a great turning point when, in the fullness of time, the first rockets rose from the earth and pierced the veil of the atmosphere?

1:4 Who among the nations could forget the day when Sputnik circled the globe, and did not every heart tremble with both wonder and fear at the sound of its signal?

1:5 Did not Yuri Gagarin become the first of all people to journey beyond the earth, and was he not forever changed by the sight of our world suspended in the darkness?

1:6 Was it not the Americans who declared that they would send their sons to the moon, and did not the whole earth watch in breathless anticipation?

1:7 Who can measure the courage it took for Neil Armstrong to set his foot upon the lunar dust, and who can forget the words he spoke for all of humanity to hear?

1:8 Have not the rovers that crawled across the red soil of Mars revealed to us that water once flowed upon its surface, and does this not stir the question of whether life once flourished there?

Chapter 2

2:1 Do not the great telescopes, such as Hubble and James Webb, peer into the farthest reaches of the cosmos and show us galaxies beyond number, each one a testament to the immensity of creation?

2:2 Have we not discovered thousands of planets orbiting distant stars, and does not each discovery renew the ancient question of whether we are alone in the universe?

2:3 Is it not remarkable that the Voyager probes, launched so many decades ago, still send their faint whispers back to us from beyond the boundary of the sun's dominion?

2:4 Should we not ask what drives humanity to spend its treasure and risk its children in the pursuit of the unknown reaches of space?

2:5 Is it not the same restless spirit that once compelled explorers to cross uncharted oceans and scale unconquered mountains?

2:6 Could the resources of the asteroids and the moons not sustain future generations, and might not the mining of these celestial bodies preserve the earth from further desolation?

2:7 Will not the establishment of colonies on the moon and on Mars serve as a safeguard for the survival of the human race, should calamity befall our home world?

Chapter 3

3:1 Does not the International Space Station stand as a sign that nations which contend with one another on the earth can labor together in the heavens toward a common purpose?

3:2 Have not astronauts from every corner of the world spoken with one voice, declaring that from above, no borders can be seen, and that the earth appears as one?

3:3 Is it not true that the technologies born of the space programs—from medical devices to purification of water—have blessed the lives of countless people who have never left the ground?

3:4 Can we ignore the dangers that threaten those who venture into the void, the radiation that assails the body, the isolation that weighs upon the mind, and the distances that separate the traveler from all who are beloved?

3:5 Should we not ask whether private companies, now rising alongside the governments of nations, will open the way for ordinary people to journey among the stars?

3:6 And in the generations yet to come, will not our descendants look back upon this age and ask whether we were bold enough, whether we seized the moment that was given to us?

3:7 For is not space exploration the grandest of all endeavors, the one pursuit that calls forth the fullness of human ingenuity, courage, and hope?

3:8 And does it not compel us, above all, to ask the deepest question of all: what is our place and our purpose among the vast and enduring expanse of the stars?
