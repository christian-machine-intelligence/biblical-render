Chapter 1

1:1 Hearken now, and incline thine ear, for herein are set forth three reasons wherefore the treasuries of the people ought no longer to pour forth their silver and their gold unto the public houses of books and learning.

1:2 First: Behold, the age of parchment and of scrolls hath passed away, and the age of the luminous screen hath come upon the land; for in these latter days, the knowledge that was once bound between covers of leather and of cloth is now made manifest upon the devices which every man, woman, and child doth carry in the palm of the hand.

1:3 The writings and the records, the treatises and the chronicles, yea, even the stories and the tales of old, are now obtainable through the waters of the great digital deep, and there remaineth no necessity for buildings of brick and of stone to house that which dwelleth already in the ether.

1:4 Second: The coffers of the cities and of the counties groaneth under the weight of many burdens, and the gold which is rendered unto the public libraries is gold which might be rendered unto other purposes of greater urgency—unto the healing of the sick, unto the mending of the roads and the bridges, and unto the safekeeping of the people.

1:5 For the stewards of the public purse must choose wisely where their portion shall be given, and a house of books is not meet to be set above the necessities of life and of well-being.

1:6 Third: That which the government provideth freely, the merchant and the tradesman cannot sell; and so it cometh to pass that the public houses of books do stand as a stumbling block before the booksellers and the makers of knowledge in the marketplace, preventing the hand of commerce from flourishing in its fullness.

1:7 For if a man receiveth bread without cost, wherefore shall he buy bread from the baker? Even so, the lending of books without price doth diminish the trade of those who labor to sell books and learning unto the people.
